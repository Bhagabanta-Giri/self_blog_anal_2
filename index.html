Introduction

A System Under Construction

Most people describe themselves through labels.

Student. Engineer. Gamer. Writer.

But labels are static — and I don't see myself as something static.

I see myself as a system under construction.

A system that is constantly being designed, tested, broken, and rebuilt. A system that evolves with time, experience, and failure. A system that is not defined by where it currently stands, but by how effectively it adapts and improves.

My name is Bhagabanta Giri, and I am currently pursuing Biomedical Engineering at NIT Rourkela. But that is just the entry point, not the definition.

What truly defines me is how I approach life.

I don't just go through things, learn or plan — I analyze them, try to understand the underlying structure and build systems that can sustain those plans even when motivation disappears. Because motivation is unreliable. Systems are not.

This blog is not a record of achievements. It is a documentation of a process.

Origins

Early Life & Foundations

I was born in Baripada, Odisha, in May 2008. From there, my life moved across multiple districts as my father's government job required frequent relocation. For the most part, I grew up in tribal districts like Koraput, Kandhamala, and Rayagada.

Some of my earliest memories are from Kandhamal district — Phulbani and G. Udayagiri — where I lived between the ages of three and eight. The houses we lived in often had no running water or electricity.

But strangely, I never experienced it as hardship.

It was a childhood defined by curiosity, simplicity, and contentment. Looking back, it feels like a slow and natural unfolding rather than a struggle.

One of the most vivid memories from my childhood is my first visit to Bhubaneswar. It wasn't just a trip — it was an experience. The train journey itself felt significant. And when I reached the city, everything seemed larger than life:

  • Well-laid roads
  • Electrified houses
  • A massive overhead water tank
  • A huge library
  • Small shops selling colorful drinks and sweets

It felt like stepping into a completely different world.

Philosophy

The Origin of My Thinking

There wasn't a single defining moment that shaped me. No sudden realization. No dramatic turning point.

Instead, it was something quieter: curiosity combined with observation.

From an early age, I found it difficult to accept things blindly. Whenever something was presented as "the right way," I would instinctively question:

Why is this the right way?
In what context does this work?
Where does it fail?

Over time, this developed into a personal filter. I realized that:

  • Not all advice is useful
  • Not all knowledge is applicable
  • Not all experiences translate across situations

This led me to a rule I still follow:

Never give or accept advice unless you understand the circumstance, the experience, and the observation behind it.

This principle helps me filter noise and focus on what actually matters. Because most mistakes don't come from lack of knowledge — but from misapplied knowledge.

How I Learn

One of the biggest shifts in my learning approach has been moving away from passive consumption.

I prefer
  • Documentation over tutorials
  • Codebases over explanations
  • Implementation over observation
Why

Watching only "feels" like learning. Real learning happens when something doesn't work — when you're forced to figure it out yourself. That's where growth happens.

A Normal Day

My day is loosely divided into:

  • Learning blocks
  • Building blocks
  • Low-effort tasks

This ensures that even on low-energy days, progress continues.

Using JARVIS as a Control Layer

I use my own system — JARVIS — as a central interface for task tracking, routine management, reminders, and logs.

How I Handle Work

Work is approached in a 5-step cycle:

01Understand
02Break Down
03Execute
04Debug
05Refine

Dealing with Problems

When something doesn't work, the approach is systematic:

  • Identify the exact point of failure
  • Reduce the problem to a smaller test case
  • Check assumptions
  • Fix one variable at a time

Handling Low-Productivity Days

Not every day is optimal. On such days, urgency and importance take the backseat — priority is handled by task size and ease. Easier and smaller tasks have higher priority. Momentum is maintained, even if output is low.

End of Day Reflection

At the end of the day, I review what was planned, what was actually done, and where time was lost. This is not for guilt. It is for adjustment of tasks for the next day.

Systems in Practice

Projects

JARVIS — A Personal Intelligence System

JARVIS is a personal assistant system that I am building to understand and adapt to user behavior over time. The idea is simple: instead of a static assistant that only responds to commands, JARVIS is designed to learn from interactions and improve continuously.

What it isA Telegram-based assistant that acts as a central system for managing tasks, tracking habits, and organizing daily routines.
How it works Takes input through commands and messages · Stores and analyzes patterns in behavior · Uses data to suggest routines, reminders, and improvements · Continuously updates responses based on past interactions.
Why I made itMost tools require constant manual input and don't adapt to the user. I wanted to build something that reduces decision fatigue and becomes more useful the more it is used.
What it solves Lack of personalized productivity systems · Repetitive manual planning · Difficulty maintaining consistency.
Planned AI/ML integration · Cross-platform support · Smarter habit tracking · Voice interaction and automation.

Line Following Bot

A robotics project focused on building a bot that can follow a predefined path using sensors.

What it isA small autonomous robot that detects and follows a line on the ground.
How it worksIR sensors detect contrast between line and surface · Signals sent to microcontroller · Controller adjusts motor speed and direction to stay on track.
Why I made itTo understand the basics of robotics, sensor integration, and real-time decision-making.
PlannedBetter accuracy and speed control · Obstacle detection · More efficient path correction algorithms.

Kloom (Vision X)

How it worksAuthentication systems for user accounts · Database-backed data storage · Backend logic for requests.

Ambitions

Modular Speaker System

I want to build a modular speaker system composed of independent units that can function individually or combine to form a more powerful system. It is about understanding modular design — how small, self-sufficient systems can scale through coordination. The goal is flexibility: portable when needed, powerful when combined, adaptable to different environments. Similar ideas exist in commercial products, but the objective here is not replication — it is understanding the system from first principles.

Game Design: Decisions That Persist

I want to build a game where the player controls two characters, with decisions that directly influence the final outcome. Eventually, one of those characters becomes the final challenge. This is not just a storytelling mechanic. It is an exploration of state persistence, decision consequence systems, and long-term feedback loops. The idea is simple: every action should carry weight — not immediately, but eventually.

Mechanical Suit Concept

I want to design a basic mechanical suit capable of assisting movement or performing controlled tasks. This is not about building something cinematic. It is about understanding mechanical linkages, power transmission, control systems, and human-machine interaction. The goal is to bridge the gap between theoretical engineering and physical execution.

Mechanical Music Machine

Inspired by mechanical music systems, I want to build a machine that produces music through motion. Instead of digital sound generation, this system relies on physical interactions, timing mechanisms, and mechanical precision. It is an exploration of how mathematics, physics, and art intersect in real systems.

Ground-Level Exploration & Service

Eating in langars instead of fancy restaurants. It has no underlying reason for "building systems" and stuff. Its ambition in its purest form. Travelling India the nomadic way.

Life Beyond Systems

Practical Thinking

Most systems assume stability. I don't.

I believe in being prepared when systems fail — not because I expect a "doomsday," but because real life is unpredictable. Accidents, power cuts, network failures, natural disasters, or even small everyday breakdowns can disrupt everything we rely on.

This mindset didn't come randomly. My father emphasized it heavily — teaching me that independence is not just about knowledge or money, but about being capable when things go wrong.

These skills may sound extreme at first, but they are deeply practical:

Emergency Response
  • Bleeding control & basic first aid
  • CPR & choking (Heimlich)
  • Fire response & safe evacuation
  • Water safety & swimming
  • Shock recognition & basic trauma care
  • Mental first aid
When Systems Fail
  • Phone & power resilience
  • Signalling for help
  • Shelter & warmth
  • Water purification
  • Navigation (compass & maps)
  • Financial readiness
Everyday Independence
  • Basic vehicle & bike fixes
  • Electrical safety
  • Drilling, mounting & repairs
  • Sewing & clothing repair
  • Cooking & food safety
  • Knot tying & improvisation

These are not "survivalist" skills. They are life skills. Because real capability is revealed when convenience disappears — and when it does, preparation is what separates panic from control.

Music

Poker: Mathematics in Disguise

Poker is often misunderstood as gambling. In reality, at a deeper level, it is a system built on probability, expected value (EV), risk management, and behavioral analysis. Every decision can be broken down mathematically: pot odds, bluff frequency, long-term expectation. At scale, poker is not luck-driven. It is a decision-making system under uncertainty — similar to real-world scenarios.

Known Bugs

Weaknesses

Bug #001

Procrastination

Tasks are understood, planned, and structured… but not started on time. This creates a chain reaction:

Delay → Pressure → Compromised quality → Missed opportunities

Left unchecked, it does not waste hours. It wastes momentum.

Bug #002

Overthinking

Spending excessive time analyzing instead of acting. It creates the illusion of progress: plans improve, ideas refine — but output remains unchanged. At its worst, it replaces execution entirely.

Bug #003

Inconsistency

Periods of high focus followed by sudden drops. The system works — but not continuously. And without consistency, even strong systems fail to produce long-term results.

Bug #004

Execution Gap

Ideas are clear. Systems are designed. But execution does not always match that clarity. This is the most critical weakness. Because in the real world: only executed systems exist. Everything else is theory.
